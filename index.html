Wine for Dummies (Hospitality Edition)

Not “how to drink wine” — how to serve, describe, and sell wine like a pro, fast. Built for pre-shift brains.

1-minute Wine Framework (what you say, every time)

Describe any wine in 4 beats:

1) Style: still / sparkling 2) Body: light / medium / full 3) Structure: acid (fresh) + tannin (grip) 4) Flavor: fruit + “other” (oak, herbs, mineral)

Translation: you’re giving the guest decision-relevant info, not poetry.

Our By-The-Glass (Sell in 1 Sentence)

Staff goal: one clear sentence + one pairing. No rambling.

Esedra | Pinot Grigio — Friuli, Italy

Sell it like this: Light, crisp, and mineral with lemon and pear — super refreshing and clean.

Best for: Salads, seafood, fried appetizers, patio drinkers.

Serve 6–8°C. White glass.

Villa Maria | Sauvignon Blanc — Marlborough, NZ

Sell it like this: Zesty and aromatic — lime, grapefruit, and tropical fruit with a sharp fresh snap.

Best for: Oysters, goat cheese, citrus dishes, anything bright.

Serve 6–8°C. White glass.

3XP | Chardonnay — Niagara, Canada

Sell it like this: Smooth but still fresh — apple and lemon with a soft creamy finish.

Best for: Chicken, salmon, creamy sauces.

Serve 8–10°C. White glass.

Leyda Reserve | Pinot Noir — Chile

Sell it like this: Silky and easy — red berries with a touch of spice, not heavy.

Best for: Salmon, duck, mushroom dishes, guests who “don’t like big reds.”

Serve 14–16°C. Burgundy glass.

Pago Aylés “Unexpected” | Garnacha — Spain

Sell it like this: Juicy and smooth — ripe red fruit with a warm little spice note.

Best for: Pizza, burgers, tapas, group-friendly tables.

Serve 16–18°C. Large red glass.

Don David Reserve | Cabernet Sauvignon — Argentina

Sell it like this: Bold and classic — dark fruit, a touch of oak, firm dry finish.

Best for: Steak, lamb, heavier mains.

Serve 17–18°C. Large red glass. Decant optional.

Training Drill: Staff must be able to answer:

  • “What’s your lightest red?” → Leyda Pinot Noir
  • “What’s the boldest red?” → Don David Cabernet
  • “What’s the crispest white?” → Sauvignon Blanc
  • “What’s the smooth white?” → Chardonnay

Service Standards

Temps (fast + safe)

  • Sparkling: cold (think “fridge + a bit”).
  • Whites / Rosé: cold, but not numb.
  • Light reds: cool (a short chill helps).
  • Big reds: “cellar cool” not room-temp sauna.

Pro move If a red seems hot/alcoholic, a quick chill can fix the guest experience without changing the wine list.

Pours + presentation

  • Show label, confirm vintage if relevant, then pour.
  • Don’t fill the glass “to be generous” — it kills aroma and looks sloppy.
  • If a guest is paying real money: offer a taste first (house standards vary).

Guest Questions (scriptable)

“Is it sweet?”

Ask: “Do you mean fruity or actually sweet?” Then steer:

  • Dry but fruity: “ripe fruit, not sugary.”
  • Actually sweet: recommend the specific sweet option (don’t pretend a dry wine is sweet).
“What’s smooth?”

Translate “smooth” into structure:

  • Red: low tannin + softer acid.
  • White: lower acid + maybe some oak/texture.
“What’s your recommendation?”

Ask one question: “Light and fresh, or rich and bold?” Then pick confidently.

Upselling Without Being Weird

Rule: Upsell by solving a preference problem, not by pushing price.

Guests don’t hate premium wine. They hate feeling hustled.

3 “clean” upgrade lines

  • Same style, better version: “If you like that, the next step up has the same vibe but more depth.”
  • Food match upgrade: “With your steak, this one will taste smoother because it has more structure.”
  • Occasion upgrade: “If it’s a celebration, that bottle drinks more ‘special’ without being polarizing.”

The 2-question bottle sale

  • Q1: “Are we trying to match food or just drink?”
  • Q2: “Do you like fresh/crisp or rich/round?”

Pairing Cheats (actually usable)

  • Acid cuts fat: creamy, fried → crisp/high-acid.
  • Tannin + protein: steak, lamb → structured red.
  • Heat hates tannin: spicy → aromatic whites / lower tannin reds.
  • Salt loves bubbles: salty snacks/oysters → sparkling.

Watch-out Big tannin + spicy food can feel like sandpaper.

Wine Faults: Identify + Respond (no drama)

Corked (TCA)

Smells like wet cardboard / damp basement. Fruit feels muted.

Action: Replace immediately. Don’t argue. You’re not on trial.

Oxidized

Flat, bruised apple/nutty, brownish color, tired.

Action: Replace (often open-too-long BTG issue).

Too cold / too warm

Too cold = muted. Too warm = boozy + sloppy.

Action: Adjust temp before swapping wine.
